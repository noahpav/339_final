Sleeping Bear Dunes National Lakeshore

Sleeping Bear Dunes National Lakeshore, located along the northeastern shore of Lake Michigan in Michigan’s Lower Peninsula, is a natural wonder celebrated for its towering sand dunes, crystal-clear waters, and lush forests. Spanning 35 miles of coastline, the park offers breathtaking views of Lake Michigan, serene inland lakes, and charming historic villages. A favorite destination for outdoor enthusiasts and families alike, Sleeping Bear Dunes is a perfect place to hike, swim, and immerse yourself in Michigan’s natural beauty.

What They Offer

  • Hiking: Explore over 100 miles of trails, from short nature walks to challenging climbs up the dunes. Popular trails include the Dune Climb, Empire Bluff Trail, and Pyramid Point Trail.
  • Swimming and Beachcombing: Relax on pristine beaches along Lake Michigan or take a refreshing swim in its cool, clear waters.
  • Dune Climb: Scale the iconic dunes for panoramic views of the lake and surrounding forests.
  • Kayaking and Paddleboarding: Paddle along the shoreline or on inland lakes like Platte River and Glen Lake.
  • Cycling: Ride the Sleeping Bear Heritage Trail, a 22-mile path connecting key points of interest in the park.
  • Scenic Drives: Take a leisurely drive along the Pierce Stocking Scenic Drive, featuring stunning overlooks of Lake Michigan and the dunes.
  • Camping: Enjoy a night under the stars at one of the park’s campgrounds or backcountry sites.
  • Wildlife Watching: Spot wildlife such as deer, foxes, and a variety of birds, including bald eagles.
  • Winter Activities: Snowshoeing and cross-country skiing are popular during the winter months.

Some History

Sleeping Bear Dunes gets its name from an ancient Ojibwe legend about a mother bear and her cubs. According to the story, a mother bear and her two cubs tried to swim across Lake Michigan to escape a forest fire. The cubs drowned, forming the Manitou Islands, while the mother bear rested on the shore, becoming the dunes.

The area has long been home to Indigenous peoples who relied on its abundant natural resources. European settlers arrived in the 19th century, establishing farming communities, logging camps, and shipping routes. By the mid-20th century, conservationists sought to protect the area’s unique dunes and ecosystems. In 1970, Sleeping Bear Dunes was designated a national lakeshore, preserving its natural and cultural heritage for future generations.
