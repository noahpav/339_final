Keweenaw National Historical Park

Located in Michigan’s Upper Peninsula, this park preserves and celebrates the rich history of America’s first major copper mining region. Known as 'Copper Country,' this area was once the heart of a booming industry that shaped the local culture, economy, and landscape. Visitors to the park can explore historic mining sites, engage with the stories of immigrant communities, and marvel at the engineering feats that made Keweenaw a cornerstone of industrial America.

What They Offer

  • Tour Historic Sites: Visit remnants of copper mining history, including former mines, hoist houses, and processing mills. Key locations include the Quincy Mine Hoist and the Calumet Visitor Center.
  • Heritage Sites: Explore 21 affiliated heritage sites that showcase the history of copper mining and the cultural heritage of the region, such as the Coppertown Mining Museum and the Finnish American Heritage Center.
  • Guided Tours: Participate in ranger-led programs that provide in-depth insights into mining operations, immigrant communities, and the area's natural history.
  • Hiking: Traverse trails that weave through historic mining ruins and natural landscapes unique to the Upper Peninsula.
  • Cultural Events: Attend events celebrating the traditions and stories of the communities who lived and worked in Copper Country, such as Finnish music and dance performances.

Some History

The Keweenaw Peninsula has been home to copper mining for over 7,000 years. Native American communities were the first to extract copper from the region, using it to create tools and trade goods. In the mid-19th century, industrial copper mining took off, drawing immigrants from Finland, Italy, Ireland, and other nations to work in the mines and build vibrant communities.

By the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the Keweenaw Peninsula was one of the most important copper-producing regions in the world, fueling industrial growth in the United States. However, as copper reserves dwindled, mining operations ceased by the mid-20th century. Today, the park preserves this legacy, sharing stories of innovation, resilience, and cultural heritage.

Established in 1992, Keweenaw National Historical Park encompasses numerous historic sites and partner organizations that together tell the story of life and work in Michigan’s Copper Country.
