Isle Royale National Park

A pristine wilderness gem tucked away in the depths of Lake Superior known for its rugged beauty, Isle Royale is a haven for adventurers seeking solitude and a connection with nature. This remote island park is accessible only by boat or seaplane, and with its lush trails, serene waterways, and fascinating ecosystem of wolves and moose, Isle Royale is a naturalist's paradise. Whether you're a backpacker, kayaker, or simply a seeker of tranquility, the park promises an unforgettable experience surrounded by the raw beauty of Michigan’s Upper Peninsula.

What They Offer

  • Hiking and Backpacking: Explore over 165 miles of scenic trails, ranging from short day hikes to multi-day backpacking trips. Popular routes include the Greenstone Ridge Trail and hikes to hidden coves and historic lighthouses.
  • Canoeing and Kayaking: Paddle along the island's rugged shoreline or venture into its serene inland lakes for a peaceful experience surrounded by natural beauty.
  • Camping: Immerse yourself in the wilderness with a night under the stars at one of the park's remote campsites. Backcountry camping offers a truly off-the-grid experience.
  • Fishing: Cast a line in Lake Superior or the island’s inland lakes, home to trout, salmon, and northern pike. Anglers can enjoy world-class fishing in this tranquil setting.
  • Wildlife Watching: Spot the island’s iconic moose and elusive wolves, along with foxes, beavers, loons, and other wildlife that thrive in the park's unique ecosystem.
  • Boating: Explore the waters around Isle Royale by private boat or on a guided excursion. Discover secluded bays, shipwrecks, and panoramic views of the island.
  • Scuba Diving: Dive into history by exploring shipwrecks preserved in the cold, clear waters of Lake Superior, part of the Isle Royale National Park Maritime Heritage Trail.

Some History

Isle Royale National Park has a rich history shaped by both nature and human activity...

In the 19th and early 20th centuries, European settlers and industries arrived, establishing fishing villages, logging camps, and small mining operations. However, the island's rugged terrain and isolation made large-scale development challenging. By the mid-20th century, preservationists advocated for its protection, valuing its pristine ecosystems and wilderness.

In 1931, Isle Royale was designated as a national park, and in 1980, it became a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, recognized for its unique ecosystem, including the delicate predator-prey relationship between wolves and moose. Today, the park preserves its natural beauty and cultural history, inviting visitors to explore a landscape shaped by both human ingenuity and the forces of nature.
