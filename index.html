Michigan's Six Parks

Michigan's national parks showcase the rich history and breathtaking natural beauty of the Great Lakes State. The legacy began with Isle Royale National Park, designated in 1940, which is renowned for its rugged wilderness, pristine waters, and fascinating wolf-moose predator-prey study. Pictured Rocks National Lakeshore followed in 1966, becoming the first officially designated national lakeshore in the United States, celebrated for its colorful cliffs, sandstone formations, and serene waterfalls.

Beyond these landmarks, each park offers something unique. Sleeping Bear Dunes National Lakeshore, with its towering dunes and sweeping views of Lake Michigan, was named the "Most Beautiful Place in America" by Good Morning America in 2011. Keweenaw National Historical Park preserves the stories of Michigan's copper mining boom, while the River Raisin National Battlefield Park honors the War of 1812 and the struggle for freedom. Finally, the North Country Trail spans over 4,800 miles across multiple states, offering hikers a chance to explore the diverse landscapes of Michigan and beyond.
